Key finding: Kenya recorded 1,119 accidents in June 2016 alone — an average of 37 accidents per day. With 159 of these being fatal, Kenya loses roughly 5 people per day to road accidents. Extrapolated across a full year, this suggests over 1,900 deaths annually from road accidents — a preventable public health crisis.
02. Time of Day Analysis
RQ2 — When are accidents most frequent?
Critical finding: Evening hours (17:00–21:00) are Kenya's most dangerous period with 341 accidents (30.5%). Combined with Night hours, 56.6% of all accidents happen after 5pm. This is not random — it reflects driver fatigue, reduced visibility, drunk driving after work hours, and reduced police presence after dark.

03. Victim Analysis
RQ4 & RQ5 — Who is most at risk?
Alarming finding: Pedestrians are the single largest victim group at 40.7% of all accidents — yet Kenya's roads have very few dedicated pedestrian walkways, footbridges or safe crossing points. This is a systemic infrastructure failure, not just a behavioral problem.

Victim type findings:
• Pedestrians (40.7%) — largest group. Kenya's rapid urbanisation along highway corridors means foot traffic has outgrown safety infrastructure.
• Motorcyclists (22.3%) — boda bodas dominate Kenya's informal transport sector with minimal safety regulation enforcement.
• Passengers (22.0%) — highest fatality rate at 21.5%, suggesting they are often in high-speed collisions.
• Male victims (84.2%) — reflects higher male road user proportion and dominance of male boda boda operators.
• Juveniles (8.6%) — 96 children involved in accidents during a single month signals urgent need for school road safety programmes.
04. Accident Causes
RQ3 — What causes Kenya's road accidents?
Top identifiable cause: Careless driving (code 26) at 164 accidents and speeding (code 10) at 97 accidents together account for 23.4% of all accidents. These are both fully preventable through enforcement — making them the highest-priority targets for NTSA intervention.

The Unknown problem: Cause code 98 (Other/Unknown) is the single largest category at 224 records (20%). This represents a critical data gap — one in five accidents has no identifiable cause recorded. NTSA must improve data collection at accident scenes to fill this gap, as it limits both analysis and prevention targeting.

Drunk driving (code 29) at 59 records is likely significantly underreported — field officers may not always conduct sobriety tests at accident scenes.
05. County Risk Analysis
RQ1 & RQ6 — Where are accidents concentrated?
Geographic concentration: Nairobi alone accounts for 183 accidents — 16.4% of the entire national dataset despite being Kenya's smallest county by area. The top 5 counties (Nairobi, Kiambu, Nakuru, Makueni, Machakos) account for 41.8% of all accidents, all lying along Kenya's two busiest highway corridors.

K-Means clustering finding: Using unsupervised machine learning (K-Means, K=3), Kenya's counties were grouped into risk tiers based on total accidents, fatalities, fatality rate and night accident rate.

7 High Risk counties identified: Nairobi, Kiambu, Machakos, Kakamega, Kajiado, Nyeri, Uasin Gishu. These counties should receive priority NTSA resource allocation.

Important caveat: Isiolo shows a 50% fatality rate but has only 2 total accidents — this is statistically unreliable. Small sample sizes can produce misleading rates in any dataset.
06. Machine Learning Models
RQ7–RQ10 — Prediction & Risk Modelling
What the ML models do: Five machine learning models were trained on this dataset. Three predict whether an accident will be fatal (classification). One predicts how many victims an accident will have (regression). One groups counties into risk tiers (clustering). Together they answer questions the descriptive statistics alone cannot.

Higher = better at separating fatal from non-fatal
Random Forest — Feature Importance
Which factors best predict fatality?
Best model: Random Forest achieved AUC-ROC of 0.749 — the highest of all models. However, Logistic Regression has the highest Recall at 0.719 meaning it catches 71.9% of actual fatal accidents. In a road safety context, recall matters more than accuracy — missing a fatal prediction is more dangerous than a false alarm.

Feature importance insight: The Random Forest model reveals that Cause Code (31%) and Hour of Day (24%) are the two most predictive features. This means the type of accident cause and the time it happens are more predictive of fatality than any demographic factor.
Linear Regression limitation: The R² score of -0.002 for victim count prediction indicates the model explains virtually no variance. This is a data characteristic, not a model failure — 91.6% of all accidents involve exactly 1 victim, leaving almost no variance for the model to learn from. A multi-year dataset with more diverse accidents would improve this.
07. Recommendations
Evidence-based actions for NTSA & County Governments
How these recommendations are derived: Each recommendation below is directly tied to a specific finding from the data analysis above — not general road safety advice. Every number cited comes from this dataset.
1
Deploy traffic police on Nairobi-Mombasa Highway during evening hours
The Nairobi-Mombasa corridor (under multiple name variants) is the single most dangerous road with 50+ accidents. Evening hours 17:00–23:00 account for 56.6% of all accidents. Targeted patrols during this window on this specific road would address both the highest-risk location and highest-risk time simultaneously.
Evidence: Top Road Chart + Time Analysis → Section 04 & 02
2
Prioritise enforcement of Cause Codes 10 (Speeding) and 26 (Careless Driving)
Together these two causes account for 261 accidents (23.4%) and are both directly addressable through enforcement — speed cameras, targeted patrols and public awareness campaigns. Unlike cause code 98 (Unknown), these are verifiable and actionable.
Evidence: Cause Code Analysis → Section 04
3
Install pedestrian footbridges and walkways on Waiyaki Way and Thika Superhighway
Pedestrians account for 40.7% of all victims — the largest group. Waiyaki Way (17 accidents) and Thika Superhighway (16 accidents) are the most dangerous urban roads. Dedicated pedestrian infrastructure on these specific roads would directly protect the most at-risk group.
Evidence: Victim Type Analysis + Road Chart → Section 03 & 05
4
Mandate safety equipment for boda boda operators in 7 High Risk counties
Motorcyclists make up 22.3% of victims. The 7 High Risk counties identified by K-Means clustering (Nairobi, Kiambu, Machakos, Kakamega, Kajiado, Nyeri, Uasin Gishu) should be the first targets for mandatory helmet and reflective gear enforcement for motorcycle taxi operators.
Evidence: Victim Type + K-Means County Risk → Section 03 & 05
5
Deploy the Logistic Regression model for early warning at accident response centres
With a recall of 71.9%, the Logistic Regression model correctly flags 7 out of every 10 actual fatal accidents based on cause code, location and time. Deploying this model at response centres allows officers to triage incoming accident reports and dispatch medical services faster to likely-fatal incidents.
Evidence: Logistic Regression Recall 0.719 → Section 06
6
Implement school road safety programmes targeting Nairobi and Kiambu counties
Juveniles (under 18) account for 8.6% of victims — 96 children in a single month. Nairobi and Kiambu are the top two counties by accident count. County education offices in these two counties should implement targeted school road safety curricula and safe walking routes programmes.
Evidence: Age Group Analysis + County Analysis → Section 03 & 05
7
Expand NTSA data collection to include weather, road surface and GPS coordinates
The current dataset lacks three globally-proven accident predictors: weather conditions, road surface type and precise GPS coordinates. Adding these fields to NTSA's data collection process would significantly improve the predictive power of future models and enable real-time risk mapping.
Evidence: Data limitation — 20% Unknown cause codes → Section 04
08. Limitations & Caveats
Honest assessment of the data
1 Month
Single Month Dataset
Data covers only June 2016. Seasonal patterns and year-on-year trends cannot be analysed. A multi-year dataset is needed for reliable trend modelling.
20%
Unknown Causes
Cause code 98 (Other/Unknown) accounts for 20% of records — one in five accidents has no identifiable cause. This limits prevention targeting.
14.2%
Class Imbalance
Only 14.2% of accidents are fatal. This imbalance means models can achieve high accuracy by predicting everything as non-fatal — recall must be monitored carefully.
~
Underreporting
Many rural accidents are never officially recorded. The dataset likely represents a fraction of actual accidents, especially in counties with limited police presence.
